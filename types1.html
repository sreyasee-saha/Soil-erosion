Understanding the Different Types of Soil

Whether you’re a commercial farmer or hobbyist gardener, having a basic understanding of the different types of soil is crucial for ensuring your plants thrive.

Soil can be categorized into many different types — each with its own characteristics, limitations, and benefits — but how many main categories there are depends on who you ask.

What Is Soil?

According to the Soil Science Society of America, soil is “the unconsolidated mineral or organic material on the immediate surface of the earth that serves as a natural medium for the growth of land plants.”

Soil is also formally defined as the surface matter that’s been subjected to environmental factors of climate, macro- and microorganisms, and relief conditioning.

Top soil, by definition, is the upper, outermost layer of soil. As such, topsoil is usually the top 5 to 10 inches of surface dirt and is composed of organic matter, water, air, and mineral particles.

Potting soil is a type of soil mixture of peat, loam, sand, and nutrients that help vegetables, herbs, fruit, and plants grow to their maximum potential.

Soil performs seven critical functions in an ecosystem:

  • Soil is a growth medium for plants.
  • Soil modifies the atmosphere by absorbing and emitting gases and dust.
  • Soil provides a habitat for animals and organisms.
  • Soil holds, releases, absorbs, purifies, and alters the earth’s water.
  • Soil processes recycled nutrients such as carbon.
  • Soil acts as an engineering media for constructing roadbeds, foundations, buildings, dams, and other manmade structures.
  • Soil serves as a living filter to clean water before it moves into an aquifer.

Types of Soil

Soil can be categorized into three basic types:

  • Sand
  • Silt
  • Clay

These three types of soil are considered soil separates. They are divided by particle size with clay particles being the smallest and sand being the largest.
